Abstract

What this is

Fifty photographs — an aquarium, a holiday display, and a pilgrimage through the Civil Rights landmarks of Alabama (the 16th Street Baptist Church, the National Memorial for Peace and Justice, the Rosa Parks and Dexter Avenue sites) — were opened and described by an AI. The machine sorted them, ranked them, and narrated them with the confidence of a museum plaque: deciding what was a subject and what was background, folding a reflected human face into a whale, calling a sleeping person “contrast.” Each card carries the machine’s original reading as Concept, Context, and Abstraction.

What you are expected to do

This is a hypertext, not a slideshow — there is no single correct order. When you connect two images you author a story the machine never told: the tool merges their concept, context, and abstraction into a new, emergent reading that is yours to revise. Rearrange the cards, draw connections, and write the beats between them. Using the three assigned feminist texts — Crenshaw (1991), Bailey (2011), and Cohen & Jackson (2016) — argue how machines shape identity: whose stories get told, who is classified, who is surveilled, whose bodies are read. Every path you build becomes a unique argument branching off the original.

The example seeds a claim note connected through three images (with model merged stories you can open, edit, or delete). Use Reset anytime to return to the blank archive.
